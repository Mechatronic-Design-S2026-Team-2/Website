Simulation-Based Design Verification

Before committing the final robot architecture to hardware, the team used MuJoCo and MJX to verify that the six-actuator Klann concept could produce coherent body motion, support the expected load class, and keep actuator demand within a plausible motor/gearbox envelope. This simulation work did not replace physical testing; it reduced design risk before fabrication and gave the control stack a reference model for gait behavior.

How the simulation robot model runs

The MuJoCo scene treats the body as a floating base with six actuated crank joints. At each simulation step, the gait controller advances six crank phases, converts those phases to velocity commands, and applies them through the model’s crank actuators. The multi-bar Klann loops are not simplified into a prescribed foot path; instead, equality constraints close the linkage geometry and MuJoCo solves the resulting constrained forward dynamics, including ground contact and support-wheel interaction.

This made the simulation useful for design verification rather than only visualization. If crank signs, phase offsets, support placement, or linkage dimensions were inconsistent, the simulated robot would drag, fall, bind, or require unrealistic actuator effort. The same six-phase abstraction then carried into the physical ROS/ESP32 stack, where encoder-derived crank phase is used by the kinematic observer and gait controller.

What the MuJoCo/MJX work verified

The model verified the CAD-derived linkage hierarchy, crank sign convention, all-leg phase relationships, and support strategy before machining and final integration. The XML model includes a floating base_link, six named crank joints, six velocity actuators, collision geometry, an IMU/camera site, actuator-force sensors, and loop-closure constraints for the Klann mechanisms.

MJX/JAX rollouts were used to exercise CPG-style parameterizations rather than arbitrary per-joint trajectories. The team evaluated straight, curvilinear, and early untuned gait experiments to separate basic linkage feasibility from later navigation-stack behavior.

Design conclusions carried into hardware
  • Use one independently driven actuator per leg so software can correct phase relationships instead of relying on a mechanically coupled side shaft.
  • Preserve alternating tripod relationships for baseline locomotion because they keep three or more feet in contact during nominal walking.
  • Retain auxiliary support/stability elements in the final physical architecture because the simulated and physical platform both prioritize safe rider-scale support over purely leg-only demonstration.
  • Use torque and power estimates as sizing checks, then validate the final values physically because linkage friction, fabrication tolerances, and ground contact losses are not fully captured by the model.
