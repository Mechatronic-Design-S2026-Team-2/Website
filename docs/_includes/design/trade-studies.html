Design Trade Studies

Locomotion concept

The final report compares Klann linkage, Jansen linkage, and articulated 3-DOF legs. Klann won because it provided a mechanically encoded walking path with fewer joints than Jansen and far lower actuator/software complexity than 18-DOF articulated legs. That reduced software trip risk and made payload support more credible within the class schedule.

  • Why it won: one motor per leg, robust load path, repeatable stance stroke, and reduced inverse-kinematics burden.
  • Main trade-off: the foot path is constrained by link geometry, so future smoothness improvements require mechanical redesign or higher-order linkage geometry.
Actuation concept

The final actuation choice is a closed-loop servo plus planetary gearbox, not open-loop stepper or brushed DC drive. The final design uses six 400 W DSY RS485 servo motors with M17S 17-bit incremental encoders and six TG 60 mm 50:1 gearboxes.

  • Why it won: best combined score for torque capability, closed-loop control, low-speed precision, and reliability under rider-scale load.
  • Main trade-off: RS485 transaction scheduling and single-turn encoder semantics became major integration constraints.
Compute & sensing

The Jetson Nano remained the onboard computer because the system needed CUDA-capable RGB-D SLAM while still using modern ROS 2 packages through a containerized Jazzy environment. The RealSense D415 provides RGB-D data, the WT901 provides inertial feedback, and motor encoders provide proprioceptive phase information.

  • Why it won: sufficient GPU support for the patched ORB-SLAM2 CUDA path and low enough power for onboard deployment.
  • Main trade-off: older Jetson software constraints required a split deployment: SLAM outside Docker and ROS 2 Jazzy inside Docker.
