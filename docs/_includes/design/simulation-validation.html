Simulation-Based Design Verification

Before committing the final robot architecture to hardware, the team used MuJoCo and MJX to verify that the six-actuator Klann concept could produce coherent body motion, support the expected load class, and keep actuator demand within a plausible motor/gearbox envelope. This simulation work did not replace physical testing; it reduced design risk before fabrication and gave the control stack a reference model for gait behavior.

What the MuJoCo model verified

The MuJoCo model gave a fast check that the CAD-derived linkage hierarchy, crank sign convention, contact geometry, and support strategy produced the intended gait mechanics. The XML model includes a floating base_link, six named crank joints, six velocity actuators, collision geometry, an IMU/camera site, actuator-force sensors, and loop-closure equality constraints for the multi-bar Klann mechanisms. The simulated crank actuators were bounded by a ±2π rad/s command range and an explicit actuator-force limit, so torque and power plots could be used as sizing checks rather than just animations.

This was used to catch problems that would have been expensive to discover only after machining: wrong-side crank signs, unstable support placement, unrealistic actuator demand, and gait phases that moved individual mechanisms correctly but failed when all six legs ran together.

Gait-tuning structure

The MJX/JAX trainer kept the same practical interface as the physical system: six crank phases commanded by a structured gait controller. It tuned gains around a CPG/phase model rather than optimizing arbitrary per-joint trajectories. Parameters included linear and yaw frequency gains, minimum moving/turning frequency, phase proportional gain, velocity damping, inter-leg synchronization, and roll/pitch stability feedback.

A later exact-reference harness evaluated formal tasks independently of planner artifacts. It checked forward-straight, backward-straight, forward-curvilinear, and backward-curvilinear references, with a 10 ft nominal path and a 6 in goal tolerance. The resulting tests helped distinguish linkage/gait feasibility from higher-level navigation errors.

Design conclusions carried into hardware
  • Use one independently driven actuator per leg so software can correct phase relationships instead of relying on a mechanically coupled side shaft.
  • Preserve alternating tripod relationships for baseline locomotion because they keep three or more feet in contact during nominal walking.
  • Retain auxiliary support/stability elements in the final physical architecture because the simulated and physical platform both prioritize safe rider-scale support over purely leg-only demonstration.
  • Use torque and power estimates as sizing checks, then validate the final values physically because linkage friction, fabrication tolerances, and ground contact losses are not fully captured by the model.
