We are designing an assistive mobility device for Kinematic/Kinesthetic, a live performance by the “physically integrated” AXIS Dance Company. The intent is not to “fix” disability by imitating non-disabled movement, but to treat engineered locomotion as an expressive medium—exploring unfamiliar, “species-atypical,” and/or “superhuman” movement that expands how audiences understand ability and embodiment.

User

The primary rider is a wheelchair user transported during performance. The user may sit cross-legged or in a standard seated posture and must retain a clear line of sight.

What the device must do

The robot must move a seated rider using leg-initiated locomotion (wheels may exist as auxiliary load support, but legs must initiate/control motion). It must translate forward/backward, rotate in place (zero-radius turning), and be safe, robust, and not excessively loud for live performance.

Goal

Build a leg-controlled mobility device that can transport a seated rider to a designated point within 6 inches in 1 minute, while remaining safe, robust, portable, and performance-appropriate.

Constraints & assumptions

  • Footprint: ≤ 5’ × 5’
  • Mass: ≤ 150 lb
  • Payload: up to ~170 lb (evaluation tasks at 150 lb)
  • Mobility: forward, backward, rotate in place (zero turning radius)
  • Accuracy: 6” tolerance in ≤ 1 minute
  • Runtime: ≥ 30 minutes on-board power
  • Portability: pack into ~3’ cube, ≤ 7 parts, assemble ≤ 15 minutes
  • Noise: keep normal operation under ~70 dB at 5 ft
  • Safety: E-stop + safe construction
  • Platform restriction: no laptop in final machine
Full constraints & assumptions

Environment assumptions

  • Development testbed is approximately 10’ × 10’.
  • Final demo tasks use tape-defined channels/roadways for straight and curvilinear routes.

Size, mass, payload, geometry

  • Footprint ≤ 5’ × 5’
  • Rider height 2’–3’ off the ground
  • Device mass ≤ 150 lb
  • Carry up to ~170 lb rider; final scoring tasks run with a 150 lb payload

Locomotion / motion

  • Legs must initiate/control locomotion (aux wheels/treads permitted only as support).
  • Must move forward and backward.
  • Must rotate CW/CCW in place (zero turning radius).
  • Must hit 6” tolerance within 1 minute (navigation accuracy).

Portability / setup

  • Pack into ~3’ × 3’ × 3’ shipping case.
  • Disassemble into ≤ 7 parts.
  • Assemble in ≤ 15 minutes without soldering/heavy machinery.
  • Preferred: fits through a 36” door when folded.

Power, budget, build quality

  • On-board power; ≥ 30 minutes runtime.
  • Budget guideline around ~$900 (course guideline).
  • Clean, robust build: avoid “rat’s nest” wiring; appearance matters.

Sound

  • Normal operation under ~70 dB at 5 ft (brief peaks allowed but limited).

Safety

  • No rough edges/burrs/exposed fasteners; safe around nearby people.
  • Ride should be safe without strapping the user in.
  • Emergency stop controls required.

Allowed control modes

  1. Pre-programmed path, or
  2. Remote control by an operator (20’–50’ away), or
  3. User control (e.g., joystick).

Platform restriction

  • No laptop in the final machine (okay for testing/debugging only).

Success criteria

Must-haves

  • Leg-initiated locomotion (aux wheels/treads only as support)
  • Forward + backward travel; rotate in place
  • Reach a designated point within 6” tolerance in ≤ 1 minute
  • ≥ 30 minutes runtime on-board power
  • Safe operation + E-stop + robust/clean build
Demo-oriented requirements & stretch goals

Final demo tasks (typical)

While carrying a 150 lb payload, complete taped-lane tasks such as:

  • 10’ straight within ~1 minute
  • 10’ curvilinear within ~90 seconds
  • 10’ backward straight within ~90 seconds
  • 10’ backward curvilinear within ~2 minutes

Impact / robustness

Survive an impact-style robustness test (e.g., dropped mass onto the structure) without loss of safe function.

Stretch goals / coolness factors

  • Additional expressive motions beyond locomotion (e.g., leg extension, “plié/relevé”-like behavior)
  • Variable speed capability (suggested 1–4 mph range)
  • More complex navigation (mapping/sensor fusion), safer human-aware behavior
  • Distinctive, intentional aesthetic / form factor

Why this is hard

  • High payload + low mass + compact footprint: carrying a person while staying stable and under weight/size limits drives actuator sizing, structural stiffness, and stability margins.
  • Leg locomotion + repeatable navigation: legs introduce slip/compliance and intermittent contact, which complicates estimation and consistent point-to-point performance.
  • Safety without restraints: the rider must be safe without being strapped in, pushing conservative acceleration, smooth motion, and highly reliable stopping.
  • Noise under load: footfalls, impacts, and drivetrain noise must be controlled while moving heavy loads.
  • Portability + fast setup: the machine must break down cleanly into modules with reliable connectors and repeatable alignment.
  • Robustness + aesthetics: clean wiring, protected electronics, and good-looking fabrication must be built in—not tacked on late.
